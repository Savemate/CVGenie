South African Workers Rights Guide

Understanding your basic employment rights and protections

Basic Employment Rights

  • Fair Labour Practices

    Every worker has the right to fair labour practices as enshrined in the Constitution.

  • Written Contract

    You have the right to a written employment contract outlining your terms of employment.

  • Minimum Wage

    You must be paid at least the national minimum wage (R27.58 per hour as of 2024).

  • Working Hours

    Maximum 45 hours per week (9 hours per day for 5-day week, or 8 hours per day for 6-day week).

Workplace Protections

  • Safe Working Environment

    Your employer must provide a safe and healthy working environment.

  • Protection from Discrimination

    You cannot be discriminated against based on race, gender, pregnancy, marital status, etc.

  • Protection from Unfair Dismissal

    You cannot be fired without a valid reason and proper procedure.

  • Harassment Protection

    You have the right to work free from sexual harassment and bullying.

Leave & Benefits

  • Annual Leave

    21 consecutive days annual leave (or 15 working days) per year.

  • Sick Leave

    6 weeks paid sick leave over 3 years (1 day for every 26 days worked).

  • Maternity Leave

    4 consecutive months maternity leave (can start 4 weeks before due date).

  • Family Responsibility Leave

    3 days paid leave per year for family responsibilities (childbirth, illness, death).

Common Workplace Issues & Solutions

How to handle typical workplace problems

Unpaid Wages

What to do if your employer doesn't pay you.

  • Keep records of hours worked
  • Send written reminder to employer
  • Contact Department of Labour
  • File with CCMA if unresolved

Unfair Dismissal

Steps to take if you believe you were unfairly fired.

  • Request written reasons for dismissal
  • File with CCMA within 30 days
  • Attend conciliation meeting
  • Proceed to arbitration if needed

Workplace Harassment

How to handle harassment at work.

  • Document incidents with details
  • Report to supervisor or HR
  • Follow company grievance procedure
  • Contact CCMA if internal process fails

Important Notice

This information is for educational purposes only and does not constitute legal advice. For specific legal advice regarding your situation, consult with a qualified legal professional or contact the organizations listed above.
